Financial Literacy Resources

Financial Terms You Should Familiarize Yourself With

Understanding key financial terms is essential for effective money management. Below is a list of common financial concepts that can help you navigate budgeting, investing, and overall financial planning.

For more in-depth explanations of financial terms, visit Investopedia's Financial Term Dictionary.

  • Assets: Resources owned by an individual or business that have economic value.
  • Liabilities: Financial obligations or debts owed to others.
  • Net Worth: The difference between total assets and total liabilities.
  • Income: Earnings from work, investments, or business activities.
  • Expenses: Costs incurred in the process of earning income or maintaining operations.
  • Cash Flow: The movement of money into and out of a business or individual’s finances.
  • Budget: A plan for managing income and expenses over a certain period.
  • Credit Score: A numerical representation of a person's creditworthiness.
  • Investments: Assets purchased with the expectation of generating income or profit.
  • ROI (Return on Investment): A measure of the profitability of an investment, calculated as the gain or loss relative to the investment cost.
  • Equity: The value of ownership in an asset, after all debts and liabilities are accounted for.
  • Debt: Money owed by an individual or business to another party.
  • Emergency Fund: A savings reserve used for unexpected expenses or financial emergencies.
  • Retirement Savings: Funds set aside for income after retirement, often in accounts like IRAs or 401(k)s.
  • Taxable Income: The portion of income subject to taxes, after deductions and exemptions.
  • Depreciation: A decrease in the value of an asset over time due to wear and tear or obsolescence.
  • Diversification: Spreading investments across various asset types to reduce risk.
  • Capital Gains: Profits from the sale of assets or investments such as stocks, real estate, or businesses.
  • Liquidity: The ease with which an asset can be converted into cash.
  • Debt-to-Income Ratio: A measure of an individual’s debt relative to their income, used to evaluate financial health.
  • Interest Rate: The cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage of the loan amount.
  • Compound Interest: Interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods.
